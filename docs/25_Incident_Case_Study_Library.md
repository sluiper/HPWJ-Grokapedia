# Section 25: Incident Case Study Library

**Version 8.0 – Built under strict verification protocol**  
**Purpose:** Provide real-world evidence that turns FMEA RPN numbers and Master Control Register rules into memorable training material.  
**Rule:** No fabricated company names, exact dates, or unreported injury details. Where a specific public report is not available, a clearly labeled **Composite Case** is used that accurately reflects documented industry failure modes and is mapped to the relevant MCR item and FMEA mode.

## Top-5 RPN Priority Cases (First Delivery – Verified Pattern)

### Case 1 – Inadequate Pre-Use Inspection (Highest RPN 210)
**Type:** Composite Case (based on recurring industry pattern documented in WJTA guidance and manufacturer pre-use check emphasis)  
**What failed:** A high-pressure unit was started without a complete pre-use inspection. A previously activated rupture disc had not been replaced. Overpressure occurred shortly after start-up.  
**Outcome:** Equipment damage and near-miss to personnel; production delay.  
**Root causes:** Production pressure, incomplete checklist, no supervisor verification, spare disc not available on unit.  
**MCR Item that would have prevented it:** MCR-023 (Mandatory comprehensive pre-use inspection) + MCR-009 (Rupture disc single-use + spare on unit) + MCR-024 (Supervisor verification).  
**FMEA Link:** Inadequate Pre-Use Inspection (RPN 210).  
**Training point:** The highest RPN item exists because this pattern is real and preventable. Time must be allocated for the full checklist — production pressure is never an excuse.  
**Source basis:** WJTA Industry Best Practices emphasis on pre-service checklists; NLB and other OEM operating manuals that repeatedly stress rupture disc and pre-use verification; general industry near-miss pattern.

### Case 2 – Flex Lance Back-Out / Loss of Control (RPN 200)
**Type:** Composite Case (classic high-pressure water jetting failure mode repeatedly cited in industry safety guidance)  
**What failed:** Operator inserted a flex lance into a heat-exchanger tube without a correctly installed and verified Anti-Withdrawal Device (AWD). Under pressure the lance ejected backward.  
**Outcome:** Serious injury risk / near-miss or actual contact depending on energy.  
**Root causes:** AWD missing, incorrectly fitted, or deliberately bypassed; inadequate pre-use check of tooling; insufficient supervision of Level 2 operators on flex-lance work.  
**MCR Item that would have prevented it:** MCR-019 + MCR-020 (AWD mandatory and never bypassed) + MCR-023.  
**FMEA Link:** Flex Lance Back-Out / Loss of Control (RPN 200).  
**Training point:** AWD is not optional. Verify engagement every single insertion. This is the reason flex-lance work has one of the highest RPNs.  
**Source basis:** Industry-wide recognition of flex-lance ejection as a top hazard (WJTA guidance, manufacturer AWD recommendations from StoneAge / Peinemann / NLB-style tooling, repeated emphasis in training materials).

### Case 3 – Poor SIMOPS Coordination (RPN 200)
**Type:** Composite Case (based on well-documented multi-energy interface risks)  
**What failed:** High-pressure water jetting was performed adjacent to hot work / welding without adequate physical separation, zoning, or daily SIMOPS coordination. Jet mist or spray interacted with ignition sources.  
**Outcome:** Near-miss fire / explosion risk or actual event depending on severity.  
**Root causes:** No daily SIMOPS meeting, unclear zoning, permit interfaces not managed, communication failure between crews.  
**MCR Item that would have prevented it:** MCR-028 (Daily SIMOPS coordination + clear zoning + hot-work controls).  
**FMEA Link:** Poor SIMOPS Coordination (RPN 200).  
**Training point:** Jet spray/mist can carry flammable materials or create ignition risk. Physical barriers and named coordination are non-negotiable when interfaces exist.  
**Source basis:** General process safety literature on simultaneous operations, client PTW/SIMOPS requirements (Aramco/SABIC-style), and industry recognition of hot-work / water-jetting interfaces as high-risk.

### Case 4 – Quick-Connect Disconnection Under Pressure (RPN 180)
**Type:** Composite Case (specific failure mode repeatedly warned against by manufacturers and WJTA-style guidance)  
**What failed:** A quick-connect coupling was used on a high-movement hose run. During operation the connection rotated, the latch released, and the hose disconnected under full pressure, producing a violent whip.  
**Outcome:** Serious struck-by / laceration risk.  
**Root causes:** Use of quick-connect in high-movement application, worn or uninspected latch, no preference for screw-type or face-seal connections.  
**MCR Item that would have prevented it:** MCR-007 (Prefer screw-type/face-seal in high-movement applications; inspect latches every time).  
**FMEA Link:** Quick-Connect Disconnection Under Pressure (RPN 180).  
**Training point:** Quick-connects have a known failure mode under rotation and vibration. Prefer permanent or screw-type connections where hose movement is expected.  
**Source basis:** Manufacturer safety notes and industry alerts on quick-connect risks at high pressure; WJTA-related safety discussions.

### Case 5 – Loss of Control Due to Excessive Reaction Force (RPN 180)
**Type:** Composite Case (physics-based and repeatedly cited in training and manufacturer guidance)  
**What failed:** Operator used a handheld gun/lance combination that produced reaction force well above 250 N (and/or above 1/3 body weight) without mechanical assistance. Stance was lost and the jet crossed the operator’s body or a colleague.  
**Outcome:** Severe laceration / injection injury or near-miss.  
**Root causes:** Failure to calculate or respect the three additive reaction-force controls; wrong nozzle/pressure/flow combination for handheld use; fatigue.  
**MCR Item that would have prevented it:** MCR-016 (Three additive controls – all three must be satisfied).  
**FMEA Link:** Loss of Control Due to Excessive Reaction Force (RPN 180).  
**Training point:** 250 N is not a soft guideline. The three additive controls exist because loss of stance at high reaction force produces exactly this class of injury.  
**Source basis:** Industry reaction-force formula (verified against WJTA-IMCA paper), manufacturer recommendations (StoneAge geometry guidance, 1/3 body weight rule), and the physics of high-velocity jet momentum.

## How to Use This Library in ATC Training
- Review at least these 5 cases on Day 1 and Day 3 of the 3-day course.
- For each case ask: “Which MCR item was missing?” and “How would the Pre-Use Checklist or AWD check have stopped this?”
- After any real Anabeeb near-miss or incident, add a new entry (anonymized) within 30 days and update the FMEA/MCR if needed.
