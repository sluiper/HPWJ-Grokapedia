# Chapter 10: Emergency Response, Medical Management & Trauma First-Aid

**Maximum Practical Depth for High-Pressure Water Jetting Injuries**

This chapter provides the definitive on-site and immediate medical response protocol for HPWJ operations. It is designed for nozzle operators, pump operators, supervisors, and first aiders. All personnel must be trained on this content and carry the accompanying Medical Alert Card.

**Critical Principle**: High-pressure water injection injuries often appear minor externally but can cause devastating internal damage. Delayed or inadequate treatment leads to high rates of amputation, severe disability, or death. **Time is tissue.**

---

## 10.1 Scope and Objectives

This chapter covers:
- Immediate on-site response to HPWJ incidents (especially injection injuries)
- Recognition of high-pressure injection injury
- First-aid actions that save limbs and lives
- Evacuation decision-making and communication with medical facilities
- Roles and responsibilities during an emergency
- Post-incident actions and reporting

It is aligned with the Master Control Register (particularly MCR-016 Reaction Force, MCR-020 Flex Lance Back-Out, MCR-023 Pre-Use Inspection, and MCR-028 SIMOPS) and draws from WJTA, WJA, clinical hand/plastic surgery literature, and major contractor medical protocols.

---

## 10.2 Recognition of High-Pressure Water Injection Injury

**Key Clinical Features**:
- Small entry wound (often pinhole size) that may bleed minimally or not at all
- History of high-pressure water jet contact (even if it "felt like a slap" or "sting")
- Rapid onset of severe pain, swelling, and numbness distal to the injury
- Pale, cold, or discoloured skin
- Loss of sensation or motor function
- The jet can travel along tissue planes, tendon sheaths, or neurovascular bundles far from the entry point

**Common Sites**: Hands, fingers, forearms, feet, and lower legs (most common in HPWJ work).

**Warning**: The external appearance is often deceptively minor. **Do not underestimate the injury.** Many patients and even some medical staff initially dismiss it as a simple laceration or bruise.

**Master Control Register Link**: MCR-016, MCR-020, MCR-023

## 10.3 Immediate On-Site Response (First 5–10 Minutes)

### Step-by-Step Protocol (All Personnel)

1. **Stop the job immediately** — Activate emergency stop / dump valve. Ensure the pump is shut down and pressure is relieved.
2. **Do not remove any clothing or footwear** that is stuck to the wound (can worsen internal damage).
3. **Do NOT probe, squeeze, or attempt to "milk" the wound** — this can drive fluid and debris deeper.
4. **Apply direct pressure** with a clean dressing or gloved hand if there is active bleeding. Elevate the limb if possible without causing further pain.
5. **Apply a tourniquet proximal to the injury** (between injury and heart) **if there is uncontrolled bleeding or signs of arterial compromise**. Use a commercial tourniquet (CAT, SOFT-T, etc.) if available and trained. Note time of application.
6. **Keep the patient calm and warm**. Treat for shock if signs appear (pale, sweaty, rapid pulse, confusion).
7. **Call for emergency medical evacuation immediately**.

**Critical Rule**: Even if the patient feels "okay" or the wound looks small, **evacuate to definitive surgical care without delay**.

---

## 10.4 On-Site First-Aid Actions (Trained First Aider / Medic)

- Remove any constricting items (rings, watches, tight clothing) from the affected limb **before swelling worsens**.
- Clean the wound gently with sterile saline if available; do not use alcohol or strong antiseptics initially.
- Cover with a sterile, non-adherent dressing. Do not apply tight bandages that could act as a tourniquet.
- **Broad-spectrum antibiotics** (if protocol and medic available) — high risk of infection with contaminated water.
- **Tetanus prophylaxis** update.
- **Pain management** — titrate carefully; avoid masking symptoms that indicate compartment syndrome.
- Monitor for compartment syndrome (severe pain out of proportion, tense swelling, pain on passive stretch, pulselessness, paresthesia).

**Positioning**: Keep the injured limb at or slightly below heart level to reduce swelling while maintaining perfusion. Avoid elevation if arterial injury is suspected.

## 10.5 Evacuation Decision & Communication with Medical Facilities

**Evacuation Criteria (Any one of these triggers immediate evacuation)**:
- Confirmed or suspected high-pressure injection injury
- Any penetrating HPWJ injury to hand, foot, or limb
- Increasing pain, swelling, or neurological deficit
- Signs of shock or systemic involvement

**Communication Script for Medical Facility (Use this or similar)**:

"This is a high-pressure water injection injury from industrial water jetting equipment operating at [X,XXX] psi. The external wound is small, but there is likely extensive internal damage along tissue planes. This requires **urgent surgical exploration and decompression**, not simple wound care. Please activate your hand/plastic/trauma surgery team immediately. Patient is [age/sex], [vital signs], [allergies], [tetanus status]. Tourniquet applied at [time] if used."

**Do not accept** "We will assess when he arrives" if the facility is not equipped for urgent surgical intervention. Escalate or redirect to a higher-level trauma centre if necessary.

**Master Control Register Link**: MCR-023 (Pre-Use Inspection failures often precede these incidents)

## 10.6 Roles and Responsibilities During an Emergency

| Role | Immediate Actions | Key Responsibilities |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Nozzle Operator / Injured Person | Stop work, relieve pressure, self-report injury honestly | Do not downplay symptoms |n| Pump Operator | Emergency stop, isolate pump, assist with first aid if trained | Maintain scene safety, communicate with control room |n| First Aider / Medic | Lead first-aid response, apply tourniquet if indicated, prepare for evacuation | Clear communication with receiving hospital |n| Supervisor / Team Leader | Scene control, SIMOPS shutdown if required, coordinate evacuation, notify management | Preserve scene for investigation, support family notification |n| All Personnel | Clear the area, account for everyone, support the response without interfering | Follow instructions from supervisor/first aider |

**Master Control Register Link**: MCR-025 (Communication Protocol), MCR-028 (SIMOPS Coordination)

## 10.7 Post-Incident Actions

- Secure the scene and preserve evidence (do not move equipment until authorised).
- Complete incident report within required timeframe (include exact pressure, nozzle size, hose condition, pre-use inspection status, reaction force at time of incident).
- Notify company QHSSE / management immediately.
- Arrange counselling/support for witnesses and team (these incidents are traumatic).
- Conduct root-cause analysis linked to MCR items (especially pre-use inspection, reaction force, training).
- Update FMEA and Master Control Register with lessons learned.

---

## 10.8 Never Rules — Emergency Response

- **Never** delay evacuation for a suspected high-pressure injection injury.
- **Never** probe or squeeze the wound.
- **Never** remove stuck clothing/footwear.
- **Never** assume a small wound = minor injury.
- **Never** accept delayed surgical assessment from a medical facility unfamiliar with HPWJ injuries.
- **Never** remove a tourniquet in the field unless directed by a physician (once applied for arterial bleeding).

## 10.9 Prevention Link to Master Control Register

Most HPWJ medical emergencies are preventable. Key linked MCR items:

- MCR-016: Handheld reaction force three additive controls
- MCR-019 & MCR-020: Flex lance AWD and back-out prevention
- MCR-023: Mandatory comprehensive pre-use inspection
- MCR-007: Quick-connect coupling controls
- MCR-028: SIMOPS coordination

All training and toolbox talks must reinforce these controls.
