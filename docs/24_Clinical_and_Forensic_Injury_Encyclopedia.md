# Section 24: Clinical & Forensic Injury Encyclopedia

**Version 8.0**

## 24.1 Purpose

This section expands the clinical understanding behind Chapter 10 (Emergency Response) and the physics–injury link already stated in Section 16.15. It explains **why** high-pressure water jetting injuries behave the way they do, so that operators, supervisors, and first aiders treat them with appropriate urgency.  
It does **not** introduce new medical thresholds (drug dosages, exact surgical timelines, debridement protocols, etc.) that would require clinician sign-off. Where a clinical detail cannot be verified from material already in the repository or general published medical consensus, it is flagged **[UNVERIFIED — needs medical/clinical review]**.

## 24.2 Core Clinical Reality (Already Stated in Chapter 10)

High-pressure water injection injuries:
- Often present with a **small entry wound** (sometimes pinhole size) that may bleed little or not at all.
- Produce **extensive internal damage** along tissue planes, tendon sheaths, and neurovascular bundles far from the entry point.
- Are **time-critical surgical emergencies** — delay increases risk of tissue death, infection, compartment syndrome, amputation, or death.
- Are frequently underestimated by the injured person and sometimes by first medical responders unfamiliar with industrial high-pressure injuries.

These statements are already present in Chapter 10 and are the foundation of the Medical Alert Card and the evacuation protocol.

## 24.3 Why “Small Entry Wound, Large Internal Damage” Is True

From the physics already verified in Section 16:
- Jet velocities at industrial pressures are hundreds of metres per second (see Section 16.4: 371 m/s at 10k psi, 525 m/s at 20k psi, 743 m/s at 40k psi theoretical).
- The kinetic energy is concentrated in a very small cross-sectional area.
- Once the jet breaches the skin, the water (and any contaminants, debris, or lubricating oils) is driven under high pressure into subcutaneous tissues.
- Fluid follows the path of least resistance along fascial planes, tendon sheaths, and around neurovascular structures, dissecting tissues far from the visible entry site.

This is the mechanical reason a deceptively small external wound can hide catastrophic internal disruption. The same principle is well recognised in high-pressure grease, paint, and hydraulic-fluid injection injuries; high-pressure water behaves analogously.

## 24.4 Pathophysiology Overview (General Medical Consensus)

High-pressure injection injury typically produces a combination of:
- Mechanical tissue disruption and dissection
- Ischaemia from swelling and pressure within compartments
- Chemical / foreign-body irritation (if the water is contaminated or if additives, oils, or debris are present)
- High risk of infection (industrial water is rarely sterile)
- Potential for compartment syndrome

**Clinical implication already adopted in Chapter 10:** even if the patient “feels okay” and the wound looks minor, treat as a surgical emergency and evacuate without delay.

## 24.5 Time-Critical Nature

Chapter 10 already states “Time is tissue.”  
The clinical rationale is that progressive swelling, rising compartment pressures, and infection can convert salvageable tissue into non-viable tissue within hours. Early surgical exploration, decompression, and debridement by a team experienced with high-pressure injection injuries is the standard of care in published hand/plastic surgery literature.

**No specific hour-cutoff is asserted here.** Exact decision thresholds for intervention remain a clinical judgment and are **[UNVERIFIED — needs medical/clinical review]** for any numeric timeline that is not already written into Chapter 10.

## 24.6 Common Injury Sites in HPWJ Work

From operational experience and Chapter 10:
- Hands and fingers (most frequent)
- Forearms
- Feet and lower legs
- Any body part that can be struck by a free jet, whip, or flex-lance ejection

These sites map directly to the high-RPN failure modes already in the FMEA (reaction-force loss of control, flex-lance back-out, hose whip, quick-connect disconnection).

## 24.7 Forensic / Investigative Relevance

After any high-pressure injection injury:
- Exact operating pressure, nozzle type/size, hose condition, and whether AWD / reaction-force / pre-use controls were in place become critical facts (link to MCR-016, 019, 020, 023 and Incident Library Case 2).
- The Medical Alert Card and the communication script in Chapter 10 are designed to ensure receiving clinicians understand the mechanism and do not treat the injury as a simple laceration.
- Scene preservation and accurate reporting support both clinical handover and subsequent root-cause analysis / FMEA update.

## 24.8 What This Section Deliberately Does Not Contain

- Drug dosages (antibiotics, analgesia, tetanus products)
- Specific surgical techniques or debridement timelines
- Numeric “golden hour” claims not already present in Chapter 10
- Prognosis statistics that would require a formal literature review and clinical validation

Any of the above that may be desired later must be added only after medical/clinical review and, where they create new rules, logged as MCR or procedure candidates rather than asserted here.

## 24.9 Link Back to Prevention

The clinical severity of these injuries is the reason the following existing controls are non-negotiable:
- Reaction force three additive controls (MCR-016)
- AWD mandatory and verified for every flex-lance / creeping-hose insertion (MCR-019 / 020)
- Full pre-use inspection (MCR-023)
- Never leave pump unattended (MCR-011)
- Medical Alert Card carried by all team members (Chapter 10)
- Immediate dump and stop-work authority

Prevention is always preferable to even the best emergency response.
