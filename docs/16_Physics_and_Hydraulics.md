## 16.1 Introduction to Fluid Dynamics in HPWJ

High-pressure water jetting operates on basic principles of fluid mechanics. The pump displaces a volume of water; pressure is generated by restriction at the nozzle.

Key concepts: Bernoulli’s principle, orifice flow, momentum/reaction force, cavitation, energy losses, Reynolds number, jet breakup, temperature effects, water hammer, and (at extreme pressures) compressibility.

---

## 16.2 Key Constants and Units

- Water density ρ ≈ 1000 kg/m³ (20 °C, standard)
- 1 bar = 10⁵ Pa ≈ 14.5038 psi
- 1 MPa = 10⁶ Pa ≈ 145.038 psi
- 1 HP = 745.7 W

---

## 16.3 Bernoulli’s Equation

**Full form (steady, incompressible, streamline):**

$$ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant} $$

**Simplified for horizontal nozzle (neglect elevation):**

$$ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 = \text{constant} $$

At the nozzle exit, static pressure ≈ atmospheric and velocity is high. This is the origin of the theoretical jet velocity formula.

---

## 16.4 Orifice Flow and Jet Velocity (Re-derived and Verified)

**Theoretical exit velocity (Torricelli / Bernoulli):**

$$ v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \Delta P}{\rho}} $$

**Derivation shown:**
- ΔP in Pa, ρ = 1000 kg/m³
- For 10 000 psi = 68.9476 MPa = 6.89476 × 10⁷ Pa
- 2ΔP = 1.37895 × 10⁸
- 2ΔP/ρ = 1.37895 × 10⁵
- √(1.37895 × 10⁵) = **371.3 m/s**

**Independent check:** Industry approximation V (m/s) ≈ 44.721 × √P (MPa) yields exactly the same result (44.721 × √68.95 ≈ 371.3 m/s). Source: published water-jet peening literature using the same Bernoulli-derived constant.

**Corrected Worked Values (theoretical, Cd = 1):**

| Pressure     | ΔP (MPa) | Theoretical Velocity |
|--------------|----------|----------------------|
| 10 000 psi   | 68.95    | **371 m/s**         |
| 15 000 psi   | 103.4    | **455 m/s**         |
| 20 000 psi   | 137.9    | **525 m/s**         |
| 40 000 psi   | 275.8    | **743 m/s**         |

**Real flow includes discharge coefficient Cd** (typically 0.65–0.95 for commercial HPWJ nozzles):

$$ Q = C_d \cdot A \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2 \Delta P}{\rho}} $$

## 16.5 Reynolds Number

$$ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} $$

Typical HPWJ hose flows are highly turbulent (Re ≫ 4000). Example: 1-inch ID, 80 L/min, water at 20 °C → Re ≈ 66 500.

---

## 16.6 Reaction Force (Verified Formula + Consistent Worked Example)

**Industry formula (confirmed against WJTA-IMCA conference paper):**

$$ F_r \ (\text{lbf}) \approx 0.052 \times Q\ (\text{GPM}) \times \sqrt{P\ (\text{psi})} $$

Metric equivalent: ≈ 0.745 × Q (L/min) × √P (bar).

**Consistent worked example (revised):**
0.040-inch (1.016 mm) orifice at 15 000 psi.
- Theoretical velocity ≈ 455 m/s
- Using Cd ≈ 0.85 and correct area gives flow in the range **≈ 5–6 GPM** (not the previously stated 12 GPM).
- Using 6 GPM: Fr ≈ 0.052 × 6 × √15 000 ≈ 0.052 × 6 × 122.47 ≈ **38.2 lbf ≈ 170 N**

This is within the common 250 N handheld guideline for many operators but still requires the three additive controls. Higher flows or larger orifices rapidly exceed safe handheld limits.

**Three Additive Controls (industry practice):**
1. Absolute ≤ 250 N
2. ≤ 1/3 of operator body weight
3. Barrel geometry prevents jet from crossing own feet/legs

## 16.11 Water Compressibility at Ultra-High Pressure (Re-checked)

Bulk modulus of water K ≈ 2.2 GPa.

At 40 000 psi (≈ 276 MPa):
Fractional volume change ≈ ΔP / K ≈ 276 / 2200 ≈ **0.125 (12.5 %)**. 

Thus compressibility is no longer negligible at true UHP. For most industrial calculations up to ~25 000 psi the incompressible assumption remains acceptable. Above 30–40 kpsi, velocity and energy calculations should note the correction or use compressible-flow adjustments if high precision is required.

**[Partially verified – bulk modulus value is standard; exact UHP jet velocity correction factors require manufacturer or specialist CFD data.]**

## 16.12–16.13 Temperature Effects & Cavitation

Standard: higher temperature raises vapour pressure → reduces NPSHa → increases cavitation risk. Critical in KSA summer conditions. No new numeric claims.

## 16.14 Energy Losses and Efficiency

**Claim:** Well-maintained modern HPWJ systems typically achieve 70–85 % overall efficiency from shaft power to jet kinetic energy.

**Verification:** Plunger pump hydraulic efficiencies commonly fall in the 75–90 % range in manufacturer data and industry papers. Overall system efficiency (including hose losses, fittings, nozzle Cd) is lower. 70–85 % is a reasonable practical range for good systems but is **not a hard measured constant**. 

**[Marked as typical industry range – source: synthesis of pump manufacturer curves and WJTA pumping efficiency papers. Specific units should use manufacturer performance data.]**

## 16.15 Physics and Injury Mechanisms

High jet velocity (hundreds of m/s) + small orifice area explains skin penetration and deep tissue damage. Reaction force explains loss-of-control injuries. These physical realities underpin the mandatory Medical Alert Card, AWD, reaction-force limits, and immediate medical response rules.
